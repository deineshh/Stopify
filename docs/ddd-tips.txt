1. Finding the subdomains first

You figure these out from the business perspective:

Look at all the goals of Stopify (play music, manage playlists, discover songs, etc.).

Group them into broad business capabilities — not by tech layers, but by what the user/business wants to achieve.

These groups are your subdomains.

Example for Stopify:

Playback Experience (play, pause, next, shuffle, repeat)

Personal Library (curation: playlists, saved albums, songs)

Catalog (artist/album/song metadata)

Listening History & Insights (playback history, recents)

Identity & Access (login, registration, account settings)

2. Deciding bounded contexts

For each subdomain, ask:

Is one model enough to handle all the business rules here?

Or are there multiple distinct models that shouldn’t mix?

Bounded contexts are about:

Ubiquitous Language boundaries (same word means the same thing inside the context, but could mean something different outside).

Autonomous models (changes in one don’t break the other).

Clear interfaces (context maps define how they talk to each other).

Example for Listening History & Insights:

You might split into:

History Context → stores playback history (write model)

Recents Projection Context → optimized for showing “recently played” (read model, possibly device-specific)

These are separate bounded contexts inside the same subdomain.

3. The “which goes where” rule

Start with subdomains: big picture business capabilities.

Inside each, define bounded contexts that model different aspects or are implemented differently.

Every bounded context should be placed inside exactly one subdomain.

4. When to split contexts inside a subdomain

You split when:

Different teams own the models and evolve them independently.

The same term means different things in different parts of the process.

The model changes often in one area but not in the other.

One part is read-heavy and another write-heavy, and you want different optimizations.
